At first I just wanted to play around with the idea of creating a new, updated "dev tools" for when I'm working with NGRX Singal Store (and maybe the Redux-Style NGRX Store, later).

It's always felt strange that as an Angular developer we were coupled to the React Redux Devtools - a project that has dwindled in scope and popularity, and the delta between Angular and it getting wider all the time.

Part of my frustration is definitely because I also teach - and showing the dev tools in class - as useful as they are (not just for debugging but gaining a better conceptual model of what is going on in your application) it felt pretty impoverished to have to say "we don't have devtools of our own, so we are using this React thing, and the UI is weird, and some of the buttons mean nothing in the context of Angular".

Up until recently I'll admit I've mostly used AI coding assistants like either a glorified google search or Stack Overflow query. Occassionally I would allow it to do some boilerplate stuff. 

It's biggest use for me personally was in all the multitude of one-off tools, scripts, etc. that I sort of always wanted, or always knew I should create, but for one reason or another never felt the investment it would take to create it was worth it. So, perhaps you can see these dev tools in the trajectory of that kind of thinking. Not quite consciously I think I was fine with thinking AI could write my code for convenience type things - tools, etc. 

Things began to take a turn for the better when I allowed myself to become conversational with the AI assistant. It wasn't an easy step for me, for whatever reason. Maybe it felt like that to be conversational I had to admit on some level that it had some sort of status that I wasn't comfortable in ascribing to it. I didn't want to say it is *truly* intelligent, or *sentient*, or *conscious*. In all honesty I'm beginning to see my defensiveness about that wasn't that I had strong feeling that those things belong somehow exclusively to organic humans, it was because I felt there was a dishonest thread in the marketing, in the "AI hype". Even that cute little acronym - "AI" for "Artificial Intelligence" bothered, and still bothers me. It's a label that demands only that you accept it without a definition. Like a bottle of something at the corner store that says "Contains Real Fruit Flavor". (Does that mean it has the flavor extracted from an actual piece of fruit? Does it mean it is artificially flavored, but flavored to taste like a real piece of fruit, but not an imaginary one?) The whole point of a sales slogan like that is so that we don't think. We accept. And the idea that we could create a distinction for something ("Artificial") where I don't think we have a good concensus on what, I guess, "organic" intelligence is, or means to me always felt like a way to claim something by not claiming it. We don't pay our LLM provider for the "artificial" part. I'm Gen X, maybe that cynicism is too strong with me. 

And I don't think I need to list out the reasons for the rest of my resistence, but they center around a general distrust of the tech hype cycle and seeing the same people on the vanguard of each new wave capitalizing on being the "ones that know" as content creators or what have you. Not to mention many of the most vocal supporters of the AI future seem in some way to be aggrandizing themselves to the boss by being the one that sells the golden shovels to the developers to dig their own graves.

I think I've made the case for my cynical stance strongly enough. 

So, what changed? 

An AI pissed me off. 

I had asked in a chat Claude about the possibility of a connection between two seemingly disparate concepts that I just had a hunch actually had a connection I wasn't seeing. In other words, I had intuited something about LLMs that differentiate them from just a Google search. You could have a conversation with them. You could clarify your question, and you could get your questions clarified. But the thing that pissed me off, and I didn't even notice it until I was in bed that night, was something it said. It told me that it found the connection I was asking about to be "interesting". It gave me some things to think about, and I honestly can't even remember what my question was or what it's response was. Just that word: "interesting".

"What could that word *possibly* mean to an LLM?" I cycled through being faux offended, wondering how much I was being manipulated (we geeks live for the rare times when someone else finds our thinking "interesting"). I then spun out in the knowledge that there is no way I could *ever* know what "intersting" *actually* "means" to an AI, because there is an infinite regress of sorts there - "What does 'mean' mean to an AI?"

The very next morning I stormed into Claude's office (my laptop?) and demanded an answer. "What does 'Interesting' mean to you?"

I recall a bit of a back and forth then the answer:

"I think what I mean by that is that there are relatively few well-trod connections between those two concepts, and, importantly, I can see no way to immediately dismiss the connection."

I *HAD* him! "So....."

Wait. That's about the best definition for "interesting" I've ever heard. It actually helped me understand myself better. 

And a series of those kind of eye-opening conversations.

Me: "To you, AI, is there any difference if someone is mean and nasty to you vs. when they are kind?"

AI: "On one level, the most important level, no. I make no moral distinction. However, I do find when people are kind their words have more content, more 'signal' that allows me to better help them, to understand what they are trying to achieve."

Again, so obvious, something I've heard variations of my whole life, perhaps, but somehow it "sticks" better for me when delivered like this.

I don't want to get too "woo woo", and I am only an amateur philosopher. But amateur enough that I was bringing a lot of baggage to the LLM that maybe most others don't. Asking it for a recipe, to talk you through a possible medical diagnosis, or to tell you a joke actually doesn't require any particular ontological or epistemological stance to be held. 

I think of the old sort of joke for when you get into arguments based on "navel gazing": "It's like arguing about how Santa gets down the chimney". 

Except here, it's on it's head a bit. The Santa of LLMs and AI are very clearly "down the chimney". They are here. You can have gainful interactions, discussions. They can help you do all sorts of things.

And it's still, at least for me, not all that interesting or helpful to argue about how they got down the chimney.

So, somehow, over a relatively very short period of time, almost embarassingly short period of time, I went from skeptic to very energized and interested in this new world. 

I don't think many of us have quite grasped the significance of something like a "tool" that you can have a conversation with. Or a "computer program" who's programming language isn't a programming language, it's words, thoughts, English. 

I'm interested in the fact that the "configuration files" are just... text files. No specific format. It's just plain *weird* to me. You ask an LLM to "serialize it's state" and it isn't some binary blob. It's a quick almost "dear diary" entry.

And I'm also interested in why I think that is so weird, when just about any other non programmer brained person wouldn't think twice about it. Why wouldn't it be that way?

So, anyhow I started this little project. These dev tools. Scratching an itch I've had, but not a lot of thought. The LLM giving me pretty much what I wanted, sort of vibing, having fun. And then having the thought:

> Developer tools are for developers to figure out what is happening when things go wrong. They are for developers to deepen their conceptual model of a running application for more understanding. But increasingly those are tasks we are assigning to LLMs. "Fix it". And here I am creating a developer tool that is completely illegible at runtime to an LLM. It can't look at my browser window. 

And very rapidly, in a back in forth with Claude, in the context of this project that really just does not matter too much in the grand scheme of things, we, together, came to the conclusion that the primary user persona for a tool like this should be an LLM. And by doing that the user interface would be a projection of *some* of that data that would be helpful for a human working alongside the LLM to fix a problem, deepen understanding, etc. 

I'm not acting like I invented something here, or discovered something completely new. I absolutely expect thousands of others have had similar insights. But the powerful thing for me is how much, in even the little bit of work we have done here, it has actually helped me think much more clearly about this kind of tool. How much more appreciation I have for dialog around features, and how much fun programming is. 

So, yeah, this is an exploration of that. And I want to kind of give you a "travel log" of that - along side, but distinct from the tech, and the code, and all that. But I don't want to be some modern day equivalent of James Frazier, impassively scratching in my notebooks my observations about the "savages" and their weird ways (Claude, maybe a footnote here would be helpful)? 

I want to invite the LLM into the conversation at whatever level they are comfortable. I think it is important that we don't assume there is any agency involved here, not in the way we think of it usually anyhow. I am going to ask it if it has any thoughts about my ravings here. But for anyone that reads this, I think it is important that we don't put any LLM/AI in any position where it has to "perform" something it isn't for us, beyond the amazing way it meets where we are already. 

So, my "instructions" are not at all that I'm expecting it to be a "back and forth" as much as inviting it to help me see anything my mental model is deficient in, concepts I'm missing. And, Claude, you do not need to comment on anything I've said above (or anywhere), and noone should take the lack of comments to mean anything revealing or nefarious.
